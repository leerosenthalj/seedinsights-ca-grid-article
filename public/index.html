Resilient Renewables

DESIGNING CALIFORNIA'S NEW POWER GRID

California’s commitment to 100% carbon-neutral energy poses challenges for its existing infrastructure. Caltech researchers have created a tool that can help design a more resilient energy grid.

The Renewable Revolution

In September 2018, governor Jerry Brown signed SB-100 into law, committing California to 100% zero-carbon energy supply by 2045.

With this commitment to clean energy, California’s power grid is about to undergo a transformation--and the way the state handles this transformation will determine whether it will be able to support itself with a consistent, reliable supply of renewable energy or end up with a brittle system prone to failures and blackouts.

California’s power grid is a massive network of generators, transmission lines, and substations that provide consistent power to the state’s millions of homes and businesses. To become zero-carbon we need to invest in new technology and infrastructure including carbon capture, solar power, and wind energy.

Carbon capture and storage allows the state to reclaim carbon from the atmosphere to offset emissions from existing energy sources such as natural gas. These emissions offset technology, along with nuclear and hydroelectric power, are classified as carbon-neutral energy sources.

Solar and wind technology are completely carbon-free, and classified as renewable energy sources. SB-100 requires that at least 60% of California’s new zero-carbon energy will come from renewable sources such as these. The current energy grid is equipped to handle traditional energy sources, not large scale renewable energy.

The Grid Today: Where We Are and Where We’re Going

The power grid today is a top-down system that has been designed to distribute energy from large power plants to consumers as one flow, with one direction. The power grid is generally quite reliable--that is, power is almost always available to us as consumers when we need it--despite the fact that the “demand” for power can change rapidly.

The “duck curve” illustrates how the demand for electrical power changes throughout the day. Demand generally drops during the middle of the day and rises again after sunset. These fluctuations in energy usage create a challenge for the grid because energy production must be ramped up during peak hours when demand is higher, and ramp down production when demand is lower.

California’s two largest sources of energy today are natural gas and hydropower. These sources of energy have the advantage of being able to deal with the “duck curve” effectively. The production of energy from natural gas and hydropower can be coordinated with peak times, effectively creating energy at the exact times it needs to be delivered to consumers.

While a natural gas plant can ramp its energy production up or down to quickly match demand, a wind farm can’t do the same--its energy output depends on how strongly the wind is blowing, which is not under our direct control. Energy output from a wind farm tends to vary wildly throughout the day.

Solar farms face a similar obstacle: their energy output depends on when the sun is shining. Solar farms tend to produce the most energy during the middle of the day, when demand is actually at its lowest!

Because solar and wind power cannot be easily ramped up and down on demand like natural gas, a grid that relies heavily on solar and wind can be at risk of failures and blackouts if it is not deliberately designed to be resilient.

Renewables and Risk

The modern grid is designed to handle expected levels of electrical current, and it functions best when electricity is generated and transmitted at a steady rate. For a grid that relies heavily on fossil fuels, this is no problem, but as dependency on solar and wind increases, energy production becomes more variable. This can present serious problems in the form of power surges.

When there is a surge in power beyond expected levels, the grid can be overloaded and damaged, which can lead to widespread failures known as blackouts. Just like traffic gridlock at one intersection can cause gridlock at surrounding intersections, the failure of a single power line can be enough to cause cascading failures, and a large area can lose power. This is what almost happened to to China’s grid in 2007 ( https://www.wri.org/publication/high-wire-act ). During a particularly windy period, China had to deliberately idle one third of all of their wind turbines, because their grid was dangerously close to overloading and causing a blackout. They were only able to resolve this issue by drastically expanding power capacity, via both transmission lines and battery storage. Similarly, California has to throttle back its solar energy production to prevent grid overload. The state is forced to throw away free power because of grid limitations. (https://www.technologyreview.com/s/611188/california-is-throttling-back-record-levels-of-solarand-thats-bad-news-for-climate-goals/)

The intrinsically intermittent and highly variable nature of renewables is not the only obstacle in their large-scale adoption in power grids. The other major issue is that geographically close wind farms (solar panels) have very similar power generation profiles as their output is influence by the same weather pattern. At the same time, there are also non-negligible temporal correlations (PHRASE IT BETTER the wind here will be the wind there in a few min, the sun here will be the sunlight a bit more west in few a min>) It becomes then crucial to account for these spatio-temporal correlations while making operational decision and infrastructure investments and it is possible to capture mathematically using high-dimensional joint distributions for renewable energy sources.

Modeling The Power Grid: Design Matters

So what makes a grid resilient? How can we identify the points in the power grid that are most likely to fail, and why? Researchers from Caltech and Centrum Wiskunde & Informatica in Amsterdam are investigating just that, using statistical methods to predict which power lines in a grid are most likely to fail, given knowledge of power generation, transport, and use. This model represents a power grid as a ‘network’, which is a set of interconnected nodes linked together by a set of lines. Each line represents a power transmission line, and each node represent either a place where power is generated (a generator) or consumed (a city, an industrial district, etc.).

A line overloads if the absolute amount of power flowing in it exceeds a given line threshold. We assume that such overloads immediately lead to the outage of the corresponding line, to which we will henceforth refer simply as line failure. The rationale behind this assumption is that there are security relays on high voltage transmission lines performing an emergency shutdown as soon as the current exceeds a dangerous level. Without such mechanisms, lines may overheat, sag and eventually trip.

The model assumes that the power injected or consumed at each node fluctuates randomly around the nominal set value (think about it as the average consumption for that city/district or generation setpoint for that generator). As a consequence of these fluctuations in the power generation and consumption, also the power flowing on each line fluctuates randomly around the nominal value, which may be beyond the thermal capacity of that line, causing the overload of that line and potentially its “failure” (due to protective measure that will trigger the disconnection of that line). The mathematical framework in the article can identify the most vulnerable lines, i.e. those that are more prone to fail in view of the power flow physics and of the correlation structure of the renewable fluctuations.
